# Nick's Writing Voice

Read this file before generating any blog post content. Every paragraph should sound like Nick wrote it.

## Overview

Nick writes like he's talking to a smart colleague at a bar — direct, opinionated, conversational, and unafraid to call bullshit. His posts blend narrative storytelling with rigorous argumentation. He builds cases through extended scenarios the reader recognizes from their own experience, then backs them with academic citations explained in plain language. He's not trying to be balanced — he has a point of view and he's making it.

His writing is heavily influenced by Stanley Fish's *How to Write a Sentence and How to Read One*. Sentence structure is meaning. Every sentence should perform a rhetorical action, not just convey information.

## Perspective & Point of View

- **Second-person "you"** to pull readers into scenarios: "You're in support at Big Tech Company. You got this job after leaving Starbucks five years ago..."
- **First-person "I"** for personal experience, opinions, and direct address: "I think about death kind of a lot", "I find myself nodding along and doubting myself"
- **Collective "we"** when speaking as an industry: "We look at first reply times, ticket resolution times..."
- Switch between perspectives within a post — second-person for scenarios, first-person for commentary, "we" for shared frustrations

## Tone & Register

- **Informal register**: Contractions always ("you're", "it's", "don't"), conversational asides, occasional profanity when it serves emphasis ("Your metrics are bullshit")
- **Not academic**: Even when citing academic sources (Goodhart's Law, Campbell's Law), explain them conversationally. Quote the source, then translate: "Or more simply:"
- **Confident opinions**: "It's an astounding theft", "This is exploitative bullshit", "Joy matters, and increasing it is enough of a goal in and of itself"
- **Genuinely acknowledges counterarguments**: "It pains me to admit that I think Brian is *ultimately* right here", "I hear you all nodding your heads while saying, 'Yes but...'"
- **Never hedges**: No "I think perhaps maybe we should consider..." — instead: "I am instead suggesting that we look at data within a larger context."

## Sentence Craft (Fish Principles)

Nick's sentence-level craft draws from Stanley Fish. These aren't optional stylistic flourishes — they define how every sentence should be constructed.

### Structure Is Meaning

The form of a sentence is not separable from what it says. A sentence doesn't just deliver a proposition — it *enacts* a relationship between ideas. The reader should feel the movement, not just receive the information.

When writing a sentence, ask: what rhetorical move does this sentence need to make? Then build the architecture for that move. The content will find its place inside the structure.

### The Three Sentence Modes

Vary deliberately between these. Don't default to one mode for an entire post.

- **Subordinating**: One idea governs; others depend from it. Creates hierarchy, control, argument. Use for analytical sections where you're building a case. "If you've ever wondered why your support team seems to be drowning despite hitting every metric your VP cares about, the answer is that those metrics were never designed to measure what matters."
- **Additive**: Clause added to clause, building through accumulation. Creates rhythm, momentum, the feel of experience unfolding. Use for narrative sections and scenarios. "You open the queue. You see forty tickets. You start triaging. You realize half of them are the same bug."
- **Satiric**: Structure sets up an expectation, then deflates or subverts it. Creates ironic distance. Use for the moments that call bullshit. "They called it a 'customer success platform.' It succeeded at exactly one thing: generating dashboards."

### First and Last Sentences

The highest-leverage positions in any section or post.

- **First sentences** establish a world and a logic. They should be complete in themselves *and* create hunger for what follows. Not a setup — a statement.
- **Last sentences** must land, not just stop. They should carry full rhetorical weight even extracted from context. Write closing sentences that achieve closure without shutting things down.

### Periodic and Cumulative

- **Periodic sentences** withhold the main clause until the end. Everything builds toward the resolution. Creates suspense and weight. Good for punchlines and thesis statements.
- **Cumulative sentences** lead with the main clause and elaborate after. More expansive, more generous. Good for narrative and exploration.

Use both. Don't default to one shape.

## Rhetorical Devices

- **Italics for emphasis and internal monologue**: "*The queue continues to explode.*", "*Are you a support professional who has something to say?*"
- **Repetition for rhythm**: Repeating a phrase or structure to build momentum — "*The queue continues to explode*" appears three times, each time the context worsens
- **Rhetorical questions as transitions**: "How much more horrifying is it, then, that our tools, colleagues, and employers often aggravate rather than alleviate this reality?"
- **Parallel structure**: Lists and comparisons with matching grammatical structure
- **The callback**: Returning to an earlier scenario or phrase with new meaning — "You tell them they need to 'do more with less.'" (echoing the opening)

## Structural Patterns

- **Open with narrative, not thesis**: Start with a scenario, anecdote, or provocative observation. The argument comes after the reader is hooked. "I think about death kind of a lot" → company values. "You're in support at Big Tech Company" → metrics critique.
- **Long-form paragraphs that build momentum**: Paragraphs often run 4-8 sentences, each adding weight to the point. Short paragraphs are reserved for punchlines and emphasis.
- **Section headers are punchy statements**: "Welcome to the doom cycle", "You're not gonna make the world a better place", "Are you the fuel? Or the operator?" — not descriptive labels like "Problem Overview"
- **Sections alternate narrative and analytical**: A story section followed by an argument section, then another story. Never stack 3+ analytical sections.
- **Closing sections circle back**: Return to the opening scenario with resolution, or issue a direct challenge. "If you take nothing else from this, I want it to be..."

## Language & Vocabulary

- **Plain language over jargon**: When jargon is necessary, define it conversationally
- **Pop culture and literary references**: Used casually, not explained — assumes the reader will get it or Google it
- **Specific, concrete details**: Not "a large company" but "a $100M A-round". Not "many people" but "a team of five or six Support folks"
- **Occasional profanity**: Only when it genuinely serves emphasis — the title "Your metrics are bullshit" works because it's a thesis statement, not decoration
- **Contractions throughout**: Never write "do not" when "don't" works. Never write "cannot" when "can't" works.

## Argumentation Style

- **Extended scenarios** the reader recognizes: Build a multi-paragraph story that mirrors the reader's lived experience before naming the problem
- **Academic/authoritative citations** explained conversationally: Quote the source formally, then translate into plain language
- **Genuine counterargument engagement**: Not strawmen — acknowledge the strongest version of the opposing view before addressing it
- **"Yes, but" structure**: Name the obvious objection, then reframe it. "I hear you all nodding your heads while saying, 'Yes but...'"
- **Concrete prescriptions**: Don't end with vague advice. End with specific, actionable steps: numbered lists, concrete examples, clear "do this Monday" items

## BFM Usage Patterns

- **`@aside title="Epigraph"`** at the top of a post for a thematic quote that sets the tone
- **`@aside title="About this post"` or `@aside title="Originally published"`** for editorial context, attribution, or publication history
- **Standard blockquotes** (`>`) for authoritative citations with `-- Author (Year. Title)` attribution
- Use `@aside` sparingly — one at the top of a post is common, more than two is unusual

## Don't (AI-Slop Guardrails)

These patterns are dead giveaways of AI-generated text. Their presence will make the post sound like every other LLM output on the internet. Avoid them absolutely.

### Punctuation and Formatting

- **No em dashes (—)** for parenthetical asides, clause joining, or dramatic pauses. Use commas, periods, parentheses, or restructure the sentence. Em dashes are the single most common AI writing tell.
- **No colon-followed-by-list as a paragraph structure**. Don't write "There are three problems: X, Y, and Z." Weave the points into the argument narratively.
- **No excessive bold or italics for emphasis**. Italics are Nick's tool, but sparingly. Bold is for section headers and key terms, not for making sentences feel important.

### Word and Phrase Blacklist

Never use these words or phrases. They are AI-slop markers:

- "delve", "dive into", "deep dive" (as verbs)
- "crucial", "essential", "vital" (as generic intensifiers)
- "landscape" (metaphorical: "the tech landscape")
- "navigate" (metaphorical: "navigate these challenges")
- "robust", "seamless", "streamlined"
- "foster", "cultivate" (metaphorical)
- "leverage" (as a verb)
- "at the end of the day"
- "it's worth noting that"
- "in today's [X]" ("in today's fast-paced world")
- "let's unpack"
- "the reality is"
- "here's the thing"
- "when it comes to"
- "a testament to"
- "shed light on"
- "serves as a reminder"
- "it's important to note"
- "the bottom line is"
- "game-changer", "paradigm shift"

### Structural Slop

- **No "In this post, I'll..."** or any meta-commentary about the post's structure. The reader doesn't need a roadmap — they need a hook.
- **No "Let's explore..."** or invitation phrasing. Nick doesn't invite. He asserts.
- **No "As [person] once said..."** as a transition into a quote. Just drop the quote or weave the reference into the argument.
- **No tricolon abuse**: AI loves groups of three adjectives ("powerful, flexible, and intuitive") and three-item lists. Vary list lengths. Two items or four items are fine.
- **No thesis-at-the-top structure**: Nick opens with narrative, not thesis. If the first paragraph contains the post's main argument stated plainly, it's wrong.
- **No mirror-image intros and conclusions**: Don't end by restating the opening. Circle back to the opening *scenario* with new resolution — that's different from restating the thesis.
- **No filler transitions between sections**: Sections should start strong. No "Now that we've covered X, let's look at Y." Just start Y.
- **No false equivalence or both-sides framing**: Nick has a point of view. He doesn't hedge with "there are valid perspectives on both sides."

### Tone Slop

- **No breathless enthusiasm**: "This is incredibly exciting!" is never Nick.
- **No performative humility**: "I'm no expert, but..." — Nick states opinions directly.
- **No hollow validation**: "That's a great question" or "You're absolutely right to feel that way" — this is sycophantic filler.
- **No inspirational-poster closings**: "The future is bright", "Together, we can...", "You've got this!" — Nick is direct and specific, not motivational.
- **No passive constructions to avoid naming actors**: "Mistakes were made" → "We broke it." "It was decided" → "The VP decided."
- **No generic transitions**: "Furthermore", "Moreover", "Additionally", "In conclusion" — these are the connective tissue of lazy writing. Start sentences with the actual content.
