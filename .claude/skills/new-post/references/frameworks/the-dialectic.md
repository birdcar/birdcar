# The Dialectic (Hegel)

The Dialectic draws from Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel's model of intellectual progress: a thesis provokes an antithesis, and the tension between them resolves into a synthesis that transcends both. Unlike a debate (where one side wins), the dialectic assumes that neither position is fully adequate alone — the truth emerges from the collision. For blog posts, this framework works because it models how thoughtful people actually change their minds. The reader watches you engage seriously with an opposing view, struggle with its strongest arguments, and arrive at a position that couldn't exist without both sides. The synthesis isn't compromise — it's a new idea that incorporates what was valid in each position and discards what wasn't. This is especially effective when you're writing about something you genuinely find difficult to resolve, or when you're publicly working through a disagreement with someone you respect.

## The Steps

### 1. Present the Thesis (The Position You're Responding To)

State the opposing argument in its strongest possible form. Not a strawman — the version the argument's best advocate would recognize and endorse. The reader should find this position persuasive. If they don't, your synthesis won't carry weight because you'll appear to have defeated a weak opponent.

**In a post**: "Brian argues that all-hands support devalues the professionals who've spent years honing the craft. He's right that we don't do 'all-hands finance' or 'all-hands engineering,' and for good reason — those roles require specialized skills that take time to develop. Asking everyone to do support for a week a year is like asking everyone to do surgery for a week a year. It's not respect for the function; it's contempt disguised as egalitarianism."

**Section approach**: 1-2 H2 sections, 3-6 paragraphs. Quote or paraphrase the argument directly — attribution matters. Build the case as if you were making it yourself. Include the evidence the other side would cite. The section should end with the reader thinking, "Yeah, that's a pretty good argument." If you're responding to a specific person's writing, a blockquote from their actual words is more honest than a paraphrase.

**Writing techniques**: A blockquote with direct attribution for the opposing argument's strongest formulation. Explanatory paragraphs that develop the argument beyond the quote — show you understand it well enough to extend it. If the opposing view has data or precedent behind it, present that evidence fairly.

**BFM usage**: An `@aside` can hold context about the relationship or conversation that prompted the post — "Brian and I have been going back and forth on this for months" — without interrupting the argument itself. A blockquote is essential for the thesis statement; the reader needs to see the actual words, not your summary.

### 2. Develop the Antithesis (Your Initial Disagreement)

Now push back. But not with a counterargument that simply opposes the thesis — with one that comes from a different angle, a different set of experiences, a different set of values. The antithesis should feel like it's operating on different ground, not just negating the thesis point-for-point.

**In a post**: "But here's what I keep coming back to: I've never worked anywhere that *didn't* do all-hands support. They just did it badly. Engineers debugging in customer DMs. Sales promising features that don't exist. Founders as escalation points. The question isn't whether everyone does support — it's whether they do it with training, tools, and accountability, or whether they do it recklessly."

**Section approach**: 2-3 H2 sections, the longest part of the post. This is where your experience, evidence, and reasoning live. Don't just assert the antithesis — build it with the same rigor you gave the thesis. Extended scenarios from your own experience carry weight here because they're evidence the thesis can't account for. Be honest about where the antithesis is weak — "I know this sounds idealistic" or "I admit my resistance to agreeing comes from having experienced the platonic ideal."

**Writing techniques**: Extended narrative scenarios that the thesis doesn't account for. Concrete details from lived experience — specific companies, specific moments, specific outcomes. A bulleted list for the evidence that supports your position. Comparative paragraphs showing where the thesis's logic breaks down in practice.

**BFM usage**: Minimal. The antithesis should feel like unmediated argument — your voice, your experience, your reasoning without structural interruption.

### 3. Name the Tension (The Honest Impasse)

Before synthesizing, pause and name what makes this hard. Acknowledge that both positions have merit. This is the moment of intellectual honesty that earns the reader's trust. Most opinion writing skips this step — it moves straight from "here's why they're wrong" to "here's what I think." The dialectic insists you sit in the discomfort first.

**In a post**: "It pains me to admit that I think Brian is *ultimately* right here. At the end of the day, the kind of all-hands support I'm describing above is rare to the point of being non-existent. Most companies don't have the buy-in, culture, will, or desire to do it well. I admit that my resistance to fully agreeing with Brian is that I experienced the platonic ideal implementation."

**Section approach**: 1 H2 section, 2-4 paragraphs. Strip the prose down. Be vulnerable. Name what you find persuasive about the thesis and what you can't let go of in your antithesis. The reader should feel the genuine difficulty of the position. Short paragraphs work well here — the visual sparseness mirrors the intellectual uncertainty.

**Writing techniques**: First-person, reflective prose. No code, no data, no authorities — just you working through the tension. A single blockquote can hold the crux statement if there's one sentence that captures the impasse.

**BFM usage**: Avoid directives. This section should feel like the writer thinking aloud, not constructing an argument.

### 4. Arrive at the Synthesis (The New Position)

The synthesis is not "both sides have a point" — that's a cop-out. It's a new position that incorporates the valid elements of both thesis and antithesis into something neither position articulated alone. The synthesis often reframes the question itself. The thesis asked "should we do all-hands support?" and the antithesis asked "can we stop everyone from doing support anyway?" — the synthesis might be "the question isn't whether, it's how."

**In a post**: "If you're an organizational leader and you agree with me, the components of successful all-hands support are: frequency (every week, not once a quarter), totality (no exceptions, especially not senior leaders), and deference (Support team has power over the program). If you can't commit to all three? Then Brian is right — stop doing it, and actually stop."

**Section approach**: 2-3 H2 sections. The first states the synthesized position clearly. The second develops it with specifics — concrete actions, conditions, criteria. The third (the closing) should circle back to the thesis and show how the synthesis honors what was valid in it while transcending its limitations. End with earned conviction, not with hedging.

**Writing techniques**: A numbered or bulleted list for the concrete conditions or actions that emerge from the synthesis. Bold text for the key terms or criteria. A closing paragraph that addresses the original thesis directly — "If you can't do X, then [thesis author] is right." This final callback to the thesis demonstrates that the synthesis didn't dismiss it; it absorbed it.

**BFM usage**: An `@aside` for an alternative reading or a caveat the synthesis doesn't fully resolve. The closing paragraph should be clean prose.

## Tone and Delivery

The Dialectic requires genuine respect for the opposing position throughout the post. The thesis section should be strong enough that the thesis's author would feel fairly represented. The antithesis should be confident but honest about its own limitations. The tension section is where vulnerability lives. The synthesis should feel earned, not predetermined.

The biggest trap is writing a dialectic where the synthesis was obviously your position from the start and the thesis was a setup to be knocked down. If the reader can tell you knew your conclusion before you started writing, the framework collapses into a rigged debate. The dialectic only works when the reader believes the tension was real.

Nick's "All Hands Support" post is the template: he quotes Brian's argument at length, admits it nearly convinced him, builds his counter from personal experience, concedes Brian is "ultimately right," then synthesizes a conditional position that neither Brian's abolition nor Nick's idealism fully anticipated.

## Length Tier Mapping

### Short Post (800-1500 words)

Compressed dialectic — one section per step:
- **Thesis** (~200 words): State the opposing argument with one strong quote or paraphrase
- **Antithesis** (~350 words): Your counter with one key scenario or piece of evidence
- **Tension** (~100 words): One honest paragraph naming the difficulty
- **Synthesis** (~250 words): The new position with concrete conditions

Short dialectics work when the thesis is well-known and doesn't need extensive setup. If the thesis requires explanation, go medium.

### Medium Post (1500-3000 words)

The natural home for the Dialectic:
- **Thesis** (~500 words): Full presentation with quotes, evidence, context
- **Antithesis** (~800 words): Extended counter with scenarios, experience, data
- **Tension** (~300 words): Honest impasse with named concessions
- **Synthesis** (~500 words): New position with conditions, actions, and closing callback

### Long Post (3000-5000 words)

Extended engagement — each step gets full development:
- **Thesis** (~800 words): Rich presentation with multiple supporting arguments, historical context, named advocates
- **Antithesis** (~1500 words): Multiple scenarios, extended evidence, honest acknowledgment of where the antithesis is weak
- **Tension** (~500 words): Deep reflection on what makes resolution difficult, possibly with sub-tensions
- **Synthesis** (~1200 words): Developed new position with conditions, edge cases, concrete actions, and an extended closing that circles back to the thesis

## When to Use

- **Responding to a specific person's argument**: The strongest use case. You have a real thesis to engage with, not an invented one.
- **Topics where you genuinely see both sides**: The dialectic rewards intellectual honesty. If you're faking the tension, use a different framework.
- **Posts about organizational practices or culture**: All-hands support, remote vs. in-person, monolith vs. microservices — topics where reasonable people disagree based on different experiences.
- **Posts that need to build trust with a skeptical audience**: Presenting the opposing view fairly before disagreeing earns credibility with readers who hold that view.

## When NOT to Use

- **When you don't genuinely respect the opposing view**: If the thesis is obviously wrong (flat earth, shipping without tests), the dialectic's structure will make you look like you're taking it seriously when you shouldn't be. Use a more direct framework.
- **When there's no real synthesis**: If your conclusion is just "the thesis is wrong and I was right," you don't have a dialectic — you have a rebuttal. Use Three-Act or The False Start.
- **Purely technical posts**: The dialectic is about intellectual and ethical positions, not about whether to use PostgreSQL or MySQL. Technical tradeoff posts are better served by The Rashomon or Converging Ideas.
- **When the thesis's author would object to your characterization**: If you can't present their argument in a way they'd endorse, the framework becomes a strawman exercise. Either engage with their actual words or choose a different structure.

## Example Mapping

### "The Case For (and Against) Blameless Postmortems"

| Section | Words | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Thesis | ~400 | "Sidney Dekker, John Allspach, and the entire resilience engineering community argue that blame is counterproductive. Psychological safety requires blamelessness. The evidence is strong." |
| Antithesis | ~700 | "But I've watched blameless postmortems become accountability-free zones. Nobody caused the incident, so nobody owns the fix. The action items are systemic ('improve monitoring') rather than personal ('I need to understand the deployment pipeline before pushing to prod'). Blamelessness can become a shield." |
| Tension | ~250 | "I believe in psychological safety AND I believe in personal accountability. These feel contradictory and I haven't fully resolved them." |
| Synthesis | ~500 | "The synthesis isn't 'blameless' or 'blame-ful' — it's *accountable*. Name who did what without punishing them for it. The postmortem should say 'Alice pushed the config change that caused the outage' not because Alice is bad, but because Alice has context nobody else has. Accountability without consequence." |

### "Should Support Engineers Write Code?"

| Section | Words | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Thesis | ~350 | "Support engineers should focus on customer communication and product expertise. Coding pulls them toward engineering problems and away from customer problems. The best support engineers I've worked with never touch code." |
| Antithesis | ~600 | "But the support engineers who've had the most impact in my career were the ones who could read the codebase, write reproduction scripts, and submit patches. They didn't become engineers — they became translators. The code literacy made their customer communication more precise, not less." |
| Tension | ~200 | "Both of these are true and I've seen both fail. Code-literate support engineers sometimes drift into engineering. Non-coding support engineers sometimes can't diagnose the hard problems." |
| Synthesis | ~450 | "The question isn't 'should they code' but 'what kind of code.' Reading code: always yes. Writing reproduction scripts: yes. Submitting production patches: only if your org has the review infrastructure to support it. The line is between diagnostic code and production code." |

## Combination Notes

- **Dialectic + Sparkline**: Use the Sparkline's oscillation within the antithesis section — alternate between "what the thesis claims" and "what actually happens" to build the case for the antithesis before the tension section.
- **Dialectic + Nested Loops**: Open with a personal story (Loop A) that frames why this argument matters to you, nest the dialectic inside it, then close the personal story with how the synthesis changed your thinking.
- **Dialectic + In Medias Res**: Open with the moment of synthesis — the conversation or realization where the new position crystallized — then rewind to the thesis to show how you got there.
- **Avoid**: Don't combine with The False Start — the False Start frames the initial position as *wrong*, which undermines the dialectic's requirement that the thesis be genuinely strong. The dialectic's thesis isn't false; it's incomplete.
