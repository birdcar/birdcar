# The Montaigne (The Essay)

Michel de Montaigne invented the essay in 1580. The word comes from the French *essayer* — to try, to attempt, to test. A Montaigne essay is not an argument delivered; it's a mind at work, following a thread of thought to see where it leads. The writer begins with an observation, a question, or a feeling, and thinks through it in real time on the page. The reader watches the writer's mind move — doubling back, reconsidering, arriving at unexpected places. The conclusion isn't predetermined; it's discovered through the act of writing. This is fundamentally different from every other framework in this guide, which all begin with a known destination and engineer the path toward it.

For blog posts, the Montaigne works when the writer has something genuine to work through — a discomfort, a conviction, a question that doesn't have a clean answer. Nick's "Joy Matters" follows this pattern: it starts with mortality (not an obvious lead-in to company values), follows the thread through working hours, theft of time, Silicon Valley platitudes, and arrives at a two-word philosophy that the opening didn't announce. The reader doesn't feel argued at; they feel invited into someone's thinking. The trust this builds is different from the trust earned by rigorous evidence or prophetic authority — it's the trust that comes from watching someone be honest in real time.

The Montaigne is the hardest framework for an LLM to execute well, because it requires the *appearance* of unplanned thought while maintaining enough structure to be readable. The solution is to plan the emotional arc (where the thinking starts, what it passes through, where it arrives) while keeping the connective tissue loose, associative, and genuinely exploratory.

## The Steps

### 1. The Opening Observation (The Seed)

Begin with something concrete, personal, and seemingly tangential to any thesis. Not a hook — a genuine starting point. The reader should think, "Where is this going?" but not in an impatient way. In a curious way. The observation should feel like something the writer has been carrying around and finally decided to examine.

**In a post**: "I think about death kind of a lot, and I have for most of my life. I realize that might seem like a weird way to start a blog post about joy — or any post about company values for that matter — but it's true, and I think it's relevant."

**Section approach**: No H2 header. 2-3 paragraphs. First person, present tense. The observation should be specific enough to be vivid and universal enough to be relatable. Acknowledge the tangent — "I realize this seems unrelated" — because that acknowledgment is itself part of the essay's honesty. The reader accepts the detour because the writer named it.

**Writing techniques**: First-person, present-tense reflection. Concrete details that ground the abstraction — specific numbers, specific moments, specific feelings. The tone is conversational and slightly intimate, like the opening of a letter to a friend you respect. No blockquotes, no authorities — just the writer and the thought.

**BFM usage**: None. The seed should be unadorned. Any structural element would signal "this is a constructed argument," which is exactly the impression the Montaigne needs to avoid.

### 2. The First Thread (Following the Thought)

Pull on the observation. Where does it lead? What does it connect to? This section should feel associative — the writer notices a connection between the opening observation and something in their professional life, or their reading, or their experience. The connection doesn't need to be obvious. In fact, the less obvious it is, the more the reader leans in.

**In a post**: "21% of your total waking hours over a 76-year lifespan will be spent working, with another 33% sleeping. In total, that translates to you spending over half of your life either unconscious or laboring... It's an astounding theft."

**Section approach**: 1-2 H2 sections. Follow the thread from the seed into its first implication. Allow the prose to build through accumulation — clause added to clause, fact added to fact. The reader should feel the thought developing, not being delivered. Use data or specifics to anchor the exploration, but don't organize them into an argument yet. The writer is still turning the thing over, not presenting it.

**Writing techniques**: Additive sentence structure — clauses building on clauses, facts accumulating toward a feeling. Parenthetical clarifications that show the writer refining their own thought in real time ("Actually, this number also only accounts for time actually spent *at* work"). Rhetorical questions as genuine questions, not as transitions ("How much more horrifying is it, then, that our tools often aggravate rather than alleviate this reality?").

**BFM usage**: Minimal. An `@aside` can hold a clarification or caveat that would interrupt the thread's momentum but that the writer feels compelled to note honestly.

### 3. The Turn (Where It Gets Interesting)

The essay arrives at something unexpected. The thread the writer has been following bends toward a connection, an insight, or a conviction the opening didn't predict. This is not a "twist" in the narrative sense — it's the moment the writer discovers what they actually think. The turn should surprise the writer (or appear to), not just the reader.

**In a post**: "With that in mind, I present to you the central, animating philosophy of Yetto and its founders: Joy Matters."

**Section approach**: 1 H2 section. The turn can be a single paragraph or a short section. It should feel like arrival — quiet, not dramatic. The Montaigne's turns are understated because they emerge from the exploration rather than being engineered. A single sentence can carry the turn if the preceding exploration has built enough weight.

**Writing techniques**: Declarative statement after extended exploration. The contrast between the exploratory tone of the preceding sections and the suddenly clear, direct statement *is* the effect. Let the turn breathe — don't immediately explain or justify it. The reader needs a beat to register that the exploration has crystallized.

**BFM usage**: None. The turn needs no decoration.

### 4. The Exploration (Testing the Insight)

Now that the insight has surfaced, test it. Apply it to specific situations. See if it holds. This is where the Montaigne becomes practical without becoming prescriptive. The writer examines their newly articulated conviction by asking: what does this mean in practice? Where does it apply? Where does it break down?

**In a post**: "From a product perspective, it means asking questions like 'does it increase or decrease joy for a support professional to easily compare their ticket output to their colleagues?' and then refusing to build dashboards that allow for that comparison when the answer is 'No'... At the company level, it means rejecting the culture of 'speed at all costs.'"

**Section approach**: 2-3 H2 sections. Each section tests the insight in a different domain — product, company, personal. The tone shifts from reflective to concrete. Show specific decisions that follow from the insight. Use "it means..." or "in practice..." constructions that translate the philosophical into the operational. Be honest about where the insight is incomplete or where you're still working it out.

**Writing techniques**: Concrete applications introduced with "it means..." framing. Specific examples that demonstrate the insight in action, not as evidence for an argument but as tests of a hypothesis. Questions the writer is still working through ("32 hours? 24 hours? Let's find out."). The writing should feel generative — the insight is producing new questions, not closing them off.

**BFM usage**: `@aside` for applications that are interesting but tangential to the main thread. `@callout type="tip"` if a specific application is directly actionable. Keep it light — the exploratory tone should dominate.

### 5. The Closing Reflection (Where the Writer Lands)

The Montaigne doesn't conclude — it arrives. The final section should feel like the writer setting down the thought they've been carrying, having examined it from enough angles to know what they believe. The closing should be quiet, direct, and carry the weight of everything that came before. It should not summarize. It should not call to action (that's the Jeremiad's job). It should state what the writer now believes, having thought it through.

**In a post**: "None of these decisions or ideas are world-changing, and they're not trying to be. What they *are* trying to do is make working life a little more joyful for everyone we can. Joy matters, and increasing it is enough of a goal in and of itself."

**Section approach**: 1 H2 section or no header. 2-3 paragraphs. The first paragraph acknowledges the modesty of the insight — the Montaigne doesn't claim to have solved anything. The final paragraph states the conviction plainly, with the authority of having earned it through honest exploration. The last sentence should resonate beyond the specific topic.

**Writing techniques**: Short, declarative sentences after the longer exploratory prose. A callback to the opening observation — but now the reader sees it differently. First person: "I believe," "I think," "this is what I've come to." The closing sentence should work as an epigram — portable, memorable, earned.

**BFM usage**: None. The close should be the writer's voice, unmediated.

## Tone and Delivery

The Montaigne's tone is intimate, honest, and exploratory throughout. The writer is not performing expertise or authority — they're thinking in front of the reader. This requires:

- **Genuine uncertainty**: The writer must not know the conclusion at the start. Or at least must write as if they don't. If the reader can tell the exploration was pre-scripted, the form collapses.
- **Personal stakes**: The writer must care about the question. The Montaigne doesn't work for topics the writer finds merely interesting — it works for topics the writer finds *unresolved in themselves*.
- **Associative movement**: The essay's connections should feel discovered, not engineered. "This reminds me of..." and "I keep thinking about..." are legitimate connective tissue.
- **Honest self-correction**: The writer should revise their own thinking visibly. "Actually, that's not quite right" or "I'm overstating this" builds trust.

The biggest failure mode is the false Montaigne — a pre-planned argument dressed up in exploratory language. If the "exploration" leads efficiently to a predetermined conclusion, the reader will feel manipulated. The test: could the exploration have plausibly led somewhere else? If not, use a different framework.

The second failure mode is shapelessness. Montaigne himself was a disciplined writer — his essays have emotional arcs even when they lack argumentative ones. The seed must lead somewhere. The thread must build. The turn must crystallize. Without this hidden structure, the essay is just rambling.

## Length Tier Mapping

### Short Post (800-1500 words)

Tight personal essay — one thread, one turn:
- **Seed** (~150 words): One vivid observation
- **Thread** (~250 words): Follow to its first implication
- **Turn** (~100 words): The quiet crystallization
- **Exploration** (~250 words): One domain of application
- **Close** (~150 words): Where the writer lands

Short Montaignes work for single-insight personal pieces. The seed needs to be immediately vivid because you have no room for a slow build.

### Medium Post (1500-3000 words)

The natural home for the Montaigne:
- **Seed** (~300 words): Extended personal observation with context
- **Thread** (~500 words): Follow through multiple connections
- **Turn** (~200 words): Discovery moment
- **Exploration** (~700 words): 2-3 domains of application
- **Close** (~300 words): Earned conviction with callbacks

### Long Post (3000-5000 words)

Extended personal essay with multiple threads:
- **Seed** (~400 words): Rich opening with multiple tangent points
- **Thread 1** (~600 words): First line of exploration
- **Thread 2** (~600 words): Second line, connected to first but going somewhere different
- **Turn** (~300 words): The threads converge in an unexpected place
- **Exploration** (~1200 words): Extended testing across domains, with honest uncertainty about edge cases
- **Close** (~500 words): Mature reflection with multiple callbacks

A long Montaigne can support multiple threads that weave together. The risk is losing the reader in the associative movement — use H2 headers as wayfinding even though the tone stays exploratory.

## When to Use

- **Posts about personal philosophy or values**: "Why I believe X" posts where the belief is genuine and the reasoning is personal, not academic.
- **Posts where the writer is genuinely working through something**: Unresolved questions, uncomfortable realizations, positions the writer is still forming.
- **Posts that need to build deep personal trust**: The Montaigne builds a different kind of trust than evidence or authority — it builds the trust of witnessed honesty.
- **Posts with unconventional entry points**: When the connection between the opening and the topic is not obvious, the Montaigne's exploratory structure makes that connection feel discovered rather than forced.

## When NOT to Use

- **Posts with a clear argument to make**: If you know what you want to say and you have evidence for it, use Three-Act, the Jeremiad, or the Sparkline. The Montaigne's exploratory tone will feel dishonest if the conclusion is predetermined.
- **Technical posts**: The Montaigne's strength is personal and philosophical. Technical content requires precision and structure that the exploratory tone undermines.
- **Posts that need to persuade a skeptical audience**: The Montaigne doesn't argue — it explores. If you need the reader to change their mind, use a framework with persuasive architecture (Sparkline, Dialectic, False Start).
- **Posts responding to a specific argument**: That's the Dialectic's territory. The Montaigne is internally motivated, not externally prompted.

### "Why I Left Big Tech for a Five-Person Startup" — A Career Reflection

| Section | Words | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Seed | ~250 | A specific moment: sitting in a meeting where your opinion didn't matter and realizing you'd been in that meeting before, at a different company, with different people. |
| Thread | ~400 | The accumulation of those moments. The gap between "impact" on the org chart and impact you can feel. The specific project that made you start looking. |
| Turn | ~150 | "I didn't leave because the company was bad. I left because I couldn't tell if my work mattered, and I realized I needed to be able to tell." |
| Exploration | ~600 | What "mattering" means at a 5-person company vs. a 5,000-person company. The tradeoffs you didn't expect: less stability, more meaning, more anxiety, more ownership. Where the romantic narrative breaks down. |
| Close | ~200 | "I don't recommend this to everyone. I recommend it to the people who can't stop thinking about it." |

### "What Support Taught Me About Writing Code" — A Cross-Domain Reflection

| Section | Words | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Seed | ~200 | The first time you debugged a customer's problem by reading the source code. The specific feeling of understanding the code *through* the customer's experience. |
| Thread | ~400 | Support professionals develop a specific kind of code literacy: reading code to understand behavior, not to modify it. This is a different skill than engineering. |
| Turn | ~100 | "The best code I've ever written was code I wrote *as a support engineer*, not *despite being one*." |
| Exploration | ~500 | Reproduction scripts as a form of technical writing. Customer-facing docs as API design. The support engineer's mental model of software: user-backward, not architecture-forward. |
| Close | ~150 | "Support didn't teach me to code. It taught me who the code is for." |

## Combination Notes

- **Montaigne + Nested Loops**: The Montaigne's threads can serve as nested stories. Open a personal observation (Loop A), nest a professional example (Loop B), nest the core insight (Loop C), and close them in reverse. The exploratory tone softens the Nested Loops' structural complexity.
- **Montaigne + Kishotenketsu**: The Montaigne's turn maps to Kishotenketsu's twist (ten). Use the Montaigne's exploratory tone for the first three acts, then let the twist emerge as a natural discovery rather than a constructed surprise.
- **Montaigne + The Dialectic**: Start with a Montaigne exploration of your own uncertainty, then shift into dialectical engagement with an opposing position once the exploration surfaces the tension. The Montaigne's honesty makes the dialectical engagement feel genuine.
- **Avoid**: Don't combine with Sparkline — the Sparkline requires deliberate oscillation between states, which conflicts with the Montaigne's requirement for unplanned-feeling movement. The structural intentionality of the Sparkline would show through the Montaigne's exploratory surface.
