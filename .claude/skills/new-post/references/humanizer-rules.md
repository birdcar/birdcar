# Humanizer Rules

Authoritative anti-AI pattern checklist for the writer-editor agent. Based on Wikipedia's "Signs of AI writing" (WikiProject AI Cleanup) and Nick's bat-kol voice training. The writer-editor agent reads this file during every audit pass.

## The Core Principle

LLMs produce the most statistically likely output. Human writing is specific, direct, and sometimes awkwardly honest. When in doubt, be more specific and less smooth.

## Content Patterns

### 1. Significance Inflation
Puffing up importance with "marking a pivotal moment," "enduring testament," "setting the stage for." Just state what happened.

### 2. Notability Name-Dropping
Listing media outlets without context. Instead of "cited in NYT, BBC, FT," say what was said and where.

### 3. Superficial -ing Analyses
Tacking "-ing" phrases onto sentences for fake depth: "highlighting," "underscoring," "reflecting," "symbolizing," "showcasing," "contributing to," "fostering." Remove or replace with actual analysis.

### 4. Promotional Language
"Nestled," "vibrant," "breathtaking," "groundbreaking," "renowned," "must-visit," "stunning," "in the heart of." Use neutral, specific description.

### 5. Vague Attributions
"Experts believe," "Industry reports suggest," "Observers have cited." Name the source or cut the claim.

### 6. Formulaic Challenges
"Despite challenges... continues to thrive." Name specific obstacles with specific consequences, or remove.

## Language Patterns

### 7. AI Vocabulary
High-frequency AI words: "Additionally," "Furthermore," "Moreover," "testament," "landscape" (abstract), "showcasing," "delve," "tapestry," "realm," "pivotal," "crucial," "innovative," "cutting-edge," "game-changing," "transformative," "align with," "garner," "underscore," "interplay," "intricate/intricacies," "vibrant," "enhance," "fostering," "enduring."

### 8. Copula Avoidance
Writing "serves as," "stands as," "features," "boasts," "functions as," "represents" when you mean "is" or "has." Use the simple verb.

### 9. Negative Parallelisms
"Not only X, but also Y." "It's not just X, it's Y." "Not merely X, it's Y." State the conclusion directly.

### 10. Rule of Three
AI forces ideas into groups of exactly three. Vary to two or four items. Natural enumeration doesn't always come in threes.

### 11. Synonym Cycling
Rotating through synonyms for the same concept in adjacent sentences: "the platform," "the tool," "the system," "the product." Pick the clearest word and repeat it.

### 12. False Ranges
"From X to Y" where X and Y aren't on a meaningful scale. "From enterprise to hobbyist" is scope-signaling, not a range. Just list the topics.

## Style Patterns

### 13. Em Dash Overuse
Em dashes (—) are the single most common AI writing tell. Replace every one with commas, periods, parentheses, colons, or restructure the sentence. No exceptions.

### 14. Boldface Overuse
Don't bold every key term, acronym, or emphasis point. Bold is for section headers and rare emphasis.

### 15. Inline-Header Lists
"**Performance:** Performance improved..." Convert to prose or use real bullets without the bolded header.

### 16. Title Case Headings
Use sentence case. "Strategic negotiations" not "Strategic Negotiations."

### 17. Emojis
Remove emojis from headings, bullet points, and prose unless the register explicitly allows them.

### 18. Curly Quotes
Use straight quotes ("...") not curly quotes (\u201c...\u201d).

### 25. Hyphenated Word Pairs
Don't overstack hyphenated compounds: "cross-functional, data-driven, client-facing" in the same sentence. One or two is fine; three or more signals AI.

## Communication Patterns

### 19. Chatbot Artifacts
"I hope this helps!" "Let me know if you have questions!" "Here is an overview of..." Remove entirely.

### 20. Knowledge-Cutoff Disclaimers
"While details are limited..." "Based on available information..." Find sources or remove the claim.

### 21. Sycophantic Tone
"Great question!" "You're absolutely right!" "That's an excellent point!" Just respond directly.

## Filler and Hedging

### 22. Filler Phrases
- "In order to" -> "To"
- "Due to the fact that" -> "Because"
- "At this point in time" -> "Now"
- "In the event that" -> "If"
- "Has the ability to" -> "Can"
- "It is important to note that" -> just state it
- "It's worth noting" -> just note it

### 23. Excessive Hedging
One qualifier max. "Could potentially possibly" becomes "may." Commit to the claim or cut it.

### 24. Generic Conclusions
"The future looks bright." "Exciting times lie ahead." "Together, we can..." End with specific plans, specific prescriptions, or earned conviction.

## Blog-Specific Additions

These patterns are particularly common in AI-generated blog posts:

### Opening Slop
- "In this post, I'll..." or any meta-commentary about the post's structure
- "Let's explore..." or invitation phrasing
- Opening with a definition or background context instead of narrative
- Thesis stated plainly in the first paragraph

### Structural Slop
- Mirror-image intros and conclusions (restating the opening)
- "Now that we've covered X, let's look at Y" transitions
- "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally" as section connectors
- Colon-followed-by-list as paragraph structure ("There are three problems:")

### Tone Slop
- Breathless enthusiasm ("This is incredibly exciting!")
- Performative humility ("I'm no expert, but...")
- Inspirational-poster closings ("You've got this!", "The future is bright")
- Passive constructions to avoid naming actors ("Mistakes were made")
- Both-sides hedging ("There are valid perspectives on both sides")
